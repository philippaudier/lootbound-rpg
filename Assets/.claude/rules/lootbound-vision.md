# Lootbound — Creative and gameplay direction

## Core promise

Lootbound is a first-person procedural exploration RPG about leaving a refuge, taking measured risks, surviving an expedition, and returning with equipment that carries memories.

The player is not trying to save the world or become a legendary hero.

The player simply tries to travel a little farther than yesterday.

Every expedition is a choice.

Every safe return is a victory.

## Central idea

> Ordinary moments are the most precious.

Important emotional elements include:

* the refuge;
* familiar paths;
* sunrise and dusk;
* old equipment;
* landscapes crossed many times;
* the relief of returning;
* places that gradually become familiar;
* objects marked by previous expeditions;
* the contrast between safety and distance.

Lootbound should create attachment rather than only power growth.

## Equipment philosophy

Equipment is not disposable statistical clutter.

A weapon may become valuable because:

* it survived several expeditions;
* it broke and was repaired;
* it was found in a memorable place;
* it received a name;
* it carries enhancement successes and failures;
* it accompanied the player during dangerous returns.

Equipment reaching zero durability should generally become broken rather than disappear.

Enhancement should create tension through attachment and uncertainty, not through abusive grind or arbitrary destruction.

## World philosophy

The procedural world must serve exploration and memory.

Procedural generation is not the objective by itself.

The world should provide:

* recognizable geography;
* memorable silhouettes;
* readable routes;
* gradual increases in danger;
* places the player can learn and remember;
* controlled variation between expeditions.

A technically impressive terrain that produces poor paths or forgettable landscapes is a failure.

The world must communicate danger through:

* terrain;
* vegetation;
* wildlife;
* light;
* fog;
* sound;
* silence;
* traces;
* enemy behaviour.

Avoid relying only on UI labels or numerical threat indicators.

## Combat philosophy

Combat must be fair before being realistic or spectacular.

The player should understand:

* what attacked;
* when the attack became dangerous;
* why the attack connected;
* which decision could have prevented the damage.

Prioritize:

* clear telegraphs;
* accurate hit detection;
* readable reach;
* responsive movement;
* controlled recovery windows;
* satisfying impact feedback;
* enemy behaviours that can be learned.

Do not use imprecision as difficulty.

Do not design combat around click spam.

## Encounter philosophy

Enemies should form identifiable encounters rather than exist only as randomly scattered targets.

Each encounter should possess:

* a recognizable setup;
* a rhythm;
* readable roles;
* a distinct way of applying pressure;
* opportunities to disengage;
* a small emergent story.

Enemy intelligence should appear coherent and readable rather than computationally complex.

## Refuge philosophy

The refuge is a physical place, not merely a menu.

It should become more comforting as the player travels farther away.

Core refuge actions include:

* preparing;
* storing;
* repairing;
* enhancing;
* resting;
* remembering previous expeditions.

Every refuge feature must strengthen the contrast between safety and the outside world.

## Feature filter

Before adding a feature, ask:

> Does this strengthen the loop:
> Leave → Explore → Survive → Return → Become attached to equipment?

If the answer is no, postpone or reject the feature.

A technically interesting feature is not automatically appropriate for Lootbound.

## Scope discipline

The game is developed as playable slices.

For each slice:

* implement only the requested V1;
* integrate it into the current playable loop;
* prefer one polished example over many shallow variants;
* avoid adding adjacent systems “while we are here”;
* avoid expanding content before the underlying experience is validated.

Do not prematurely implement:

* complex crafting;
* deep survival simulation;
* multiplayer;
* MMO systems;
* large skill trees;
* dozens of weapon families;
* large quantities of affixes;
* advanced wildlife simulation;
* infinite procedural worlds;
* generic dungeon-generation frameworks.

## Final decision principle

When choosing between two approaches, prefer the one that makes an expedition more memorable, understandable, and finishable.

Emotion before technology.

The human experience before the system.

The remembered journey before technical spectacle.
